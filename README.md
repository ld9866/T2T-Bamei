# Telomere-to-telomere Eurasian pig genomes and a species-wide pangenome reveal structural diversity, evolution and limited reference bias
The domestic pig is one of the world's most important livestock species and a widely used biomedical model. However, the genomic consequences of the independent domestication histories of Asian and European pigs remain incompletely understood, partly due to limitations of previous reference genomes. Here, we present a near telomere-to-telomere (T2T) genome assembly of the Asian indigenous Bamei pig and perform a comprehensive comparison with the T2T-Sscrofa (Duroc). Comparative analyses reveal remarkable conservation of chromosome structure between Asian and European pig lineages, while identifying lineage-specific variation including structural rearrangements, centromeric architecture, and protein-coding differences. We further characterize a Robertsonian fusion involving chromosomes 15 and 17 that is specific to western European wild boars and reconstruct the evolutionary history of Eurasian pig populations using high-quality genome assemblies. To capture genetic diversity beyond a single linear reference, we constructed a pig pangenome from 107 high-quality assemblies representing global pig diversity. Using both T2T-Sscrofa and T2T-Bamei as reference backbones, we evaluated the extent of reference bias in population genomic analyses and structural variant discovery. While conventional population genetic inferences were largely robust to reference choice, the T2T assemblies recovered additional genomic sequence absent from previous references and substantially improved the representation of structural variation. The pangenome revealed extensive multiallelic structural diversity across pig populations and enabled comprehensive characterization of genome-wide variation beyond SNPs.
